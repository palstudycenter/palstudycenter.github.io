Question 2 (i) - Find the HCF and LCM of 26 and 91. Verify that Product of the two numbers = HCF × LCM.

Solution -

Prime Factorisation:

26 = 2 × 13

91 = 7 × 13

HCF:
The common prime factor is 13.

HCF = 13

LCM:
Take each prime factor with the highest power.

LCM = 2 × 7 × 13 = 182

LCM = 182

Verification:

Product of the numbers = 26 × 91 = 2366

HCF × LCM = 13 × 182 = 2366

Since,

26 × 91 = HCF × LCM

Hence, the relation is verified.

Question 4: Show that the number 6n, where n is any positive integer, cannot end with the digit 0.

Answer -

A number ends with the digit 0 only if it is divisible by 10.

Since,

10 = 2 × 5

Now,

6 = 2 × 3

Therefore,

6n = (2 × 3)n = 2n × 3n

The prime factorization of 6n contains only the prime factors 2 and 3. It does not contain the prime factor 5.

Hence, 6n is not divisible by 10.

Therefore, 6n cannot end with the digit 0.

Hence Proved.

Question 5: Show that the square of any positive odd integer is of the form 8m + 1, where m is an integer.

Answer -

Let any positive odd integer be

n = 2q + 1

where q is an integer.

Squaring both sides,

n2 = (2q + 1)2

= 4q2 + 4q + 1

= 4q(q + 1) + 1

Since q and (q + 1) are consecutive integers, one of them is always even.

Therefore,

q(q + 1) = 2m

where m is an integer.

Substituting,

n2 = 4(2m) + 1

= 8m + 1

Hence, the square of every positive odd integer is of the form 8m + 1.

Hence Proved.

Question 1: Prove that √5 is an irrational number.

Answer -

We will prove this by contradiction.

Assume that √5 is a rational number.

Then it can be written in the form:

√5 = p/q

where p and q are co-prime integers (HCF = 1) and q ≠ 0.

Squaring both sides,

5 = p2/q2

⇒ p2 = 5q2

Thus, p2 is divisible by 5.

Therefore, p is also divisible by 5.

Let p = 5k, where k is an integer.

Substituting,

(5k)2 = 5q2

25k2 = 5q2

⇒ q2 = 5k2

Hence, q is also divisible by 5.

Therefore, both p and q are divisible by 5.

This contradicts the assumption that p and q are co-prime.

Hence, our assumption is false.

Therefore, √5 is an irrational number.

Hence Proved.

Question 2: Prove that 3 + 2√5 is an irrational number.

Answer -

We will prove this by contradiction.

Assume that 3 + 2√5 is a rational number.

Then,

3 + 2√5 = r

where r is a rational number.

Subtracting 3 from both sides,

2√5 = r − 3

Since the difference of two rational numbers is rational,

r − 3 is a rational number.

Dividing both sides by 2,

√5 = (r − 3)/2

The quotient of two rational numbers is also rational.

Thus, √5 becomes a rational number.

But this contradicts the fact that √5 is an irrational number.

Therefore, our assumption is false.

Hence,

3 + 2√5 is an irrational number.

Hence Proved.

Question 1: Without actually performing the long division, state whether the following rational numbers will have a terminating decimal expansion or a non-terminating recurring decimal expansion. (i) 13/3125 (ii) 17/8 (iii) 64/455 (iv) 15/1600 (v) 29/343

Answer -

A rational number p/q (in lowest form) has a terminating decimal expansion if the prime factorization of the denominator contains only the factors 2 and/or 5. Otherwise, it has a non-terminating recurring decimal expansion.

(i) 13/3125

3125 = 55

The denominator contains only the factor 5.

Hence, the decimal expansion is terminating.


(ii) 17/8

8 = 23

The denominator contains only the factor 2.

Hence, the decimal expansion is terminating.


(iii) 64/455

455 = 5 × 7 × 13

The denominator contains prime factors other than 2 and 5.

Hence, the decimal expansion is non-terminating recurring.


(iv) 15/1600

15/1600 = 3/320

320 = 26 × 5

The denominator contains only the factors 2 and 5.

Hence, the decimal expansion is terminating.


(v) 29/343

343 = 73

The denominator contains the factor 7.

Hence, the decimal expansion is non-terminating recurring.
