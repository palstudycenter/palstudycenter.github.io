📘 Unit-2 Acids and basses
Question - Into how many categories are chemical substances divided? Name them.

Answer -

Chemical substances are divided into three categories:

  1. Acids
  2. Bases
  3. Salts
Question - What are acids?

Answer -

Acids are chemical substances that produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water.

Examples: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), and Nitric acid (HNO₃).

Question - What are bases?

Answer -

Bases are chemical substances that produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) when dissolved in water.

Examples: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Potassium hydroxide (KOH), and Calcium hydroxide [Ca(OH)₂].

Question - What are salts?

Answer -

Salts are compounds formed when the hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced by a metal or ammonium ion.

They are generally produced during a neutralisation reaction between an acid and a base.

Example: Sodium chloride (NaCl).

Question - What are monobasic, dibasic and tribasic acids? Give one example of each.

Answer -

Acids are classified on the basis of the number of ionisable hydrogen ions (H⁺) they can furnish in aqueous solution.

  • Monobasic Acid: An acid that can furnish only one H⁺ ion per molecule.
    Example: HCl
  • Dibasic Acid: An acid that can furnish two H⁺ ions per molecule.
    Example: H₂SO₄
  • Tribasic Acid: An acid that can furnish three H⁺ ions per molecule.
    Example: H₃PO₄
Question - What are organic acids? Give examples.

Answer -

The acids present naturally in plants and animals are called organic acids.

Organic acids are generally weak acids.

Examples: Acetic acid, Citric acid, Lactic acid, Tartaric acid, Oxalic acid and Formic acid.

Question - What are mineral acids? Give examples.

Answer -

The acids prepared from the minerals of the earth are called mineral acids.

Mineral acids are man-made acids and are generally stronger than organic acids.

Examples: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) and Nitric acid (HNO₃).

Question - What are strong acids? Give examples.

Answer -

An acid that completely ionises in water and produces a large number of hydrogen ions (H⁺) is called a strong acid.

Examples: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), Nitric acid (HNO₃) and Hydroiodic acid (HI).

Question - What are weak acids? Give examples.

Answer -

An acid that partially ionises in water and produces a small number of hydrogen ions (H⁺) is called a weak acid.

Examples: Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) and Hydrocyanic acid (HCN).

Question - Differentiate between strong acids and weak acids.

Answer -

Strong Acids Weak Acids
Completely ionise in water. Partially ionise in water.
Produce a large number of H⁺ ions. Produce a small number of H⁺ ions.
React rapidly with other substances. React slowly with other substances.
Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃. Examples: CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃, HCN.
Question - Write the physical properties of acids.

Answer -

The physical properties of acids are:

  • Acids have a sour taste.
  • They turn blue litmus paper red.
  • Aqueous solutions of acids conduct electricity.
  • Acids contain hydrogen ions (H⁺) in aqueous solution.
Question - Why do acids turn blue litmus red?

Answer -

Acids turn blue litmus paper red because they produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) in aqueous solution.

These hydrogen ions are responsible for the acidic nature of acids and cause the colour change.

Question - Why do aqueous solutions of acids conduct electricity?

Answer -

Aqueous solutions of acids conduct electricity because they contain free ions.

When acids dissolve in water, they produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) and negative ions, which carry electric current.

Question - Why does an acid show acidic behaviour only in aqueous solution?

Answer -

An acid shows acidic behaviour only in aqueous solution because it produces hydrogen ions (H⁺) only in the presence of water.

Without water, acids cannot ionise and therefore do not exhibit acidic properties.

Question - How do acids react with metals? Explain with an example.

Answer -

Acids react with metals to form a salt and hydrogen gas.

General Equation:

Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen Gas

Example:

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑

Question - Which metals do not react with dilute acids?

Answer -

Less reactive metals do not react with dilute acids.

Examples:

  • Copper (Cu)
  • Silver (Ag)
  • Gold (Au)
  • Platinum (Pt)
Question - How do acids react with metal carbonates? Explain with an example.

Answer -

Acids react with metal carbonates to form a salt, carbon dioxide gas and water.

General Equation:

Metal Carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon Dioxide + Water

Example:

Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + CO₂ + H₂O

Question - Why is carbon dioxide evolved when acids react with metal carbonates?

Answer -

Metal carbonates contain carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻).

When an acid reacts with a metal carbonate, the carbonate ions combine with hydrogen ions (H⁺) of the acid to form carbonic acid, which immediately decomposes into carbon dioxide and water.

H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O

Question - How do acids react with bases? Explain with an example.

Answer -

Acids react with bases to form salt and water.

This reaction is called a neutralisation reaction.

General Equation:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

Example:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Question - What is a neutralisation reaction? Give an example.

Answer -

A chemical reaction in which an acid reacts with a base to form salt and water is called a neutralisation reaction.

Example:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Question - What are monoacidic, diacidic and triacidic bases? Give one example of each.

Answer -

Bases are classified on the basis of the number of hydroxide ions (OH⁻) they can furnish in aqueous solution.

  • Monoacidic Base: A base that furnishes one hydroxide ion (OH⁻) per molecule.
    Example: NaOH
  • Diacidic Base: A base that furnishes two hydroxide ions (OH⁻) per molecule.
    Example: Ca(OH)₂
  • Triacidic Base: A base that furnishes three hydroxide ions (OH⁻) per molecule.
    Example: Al(OH)₃
Question - What are strong bases? Give examples.

Answer -

A base that completely ionises in water and produces a large number of hydroxide ions (OH⁻) is called a strong base.

Examples: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and Potassium hydroxide (KOH).

Question - What are weak bases? Give examples.

Answer -

A base that partially ionises in water and produces a small number of hydroxide ions (OH⁻) is called a weak base.

Examples: Ammonium hydroxide (NH₄OH), Magnesium hydroxide [Mg(OH)₂] and Calcium hydroxide [Ca(OH)₂].

Question - Differentiate between strong bases and weak bases.

Answer -

Strong Bases Weak Bases
Completely ionise in water. Partially ionise in water.
Produce a large number of OH⁻ ions. Produce a small number of OH⁻ ions.
React rapidly with other substances. React slowly with other substances.
Examples: NaOH, KOH. Examples: NH₄OH, Mg(OH)₂.
Question - Write the physical properties of bases.

Answer -

The physical properties of bases are:

  • Bases have a bitter taste.
  • They turn red litmus paper blue.
  • Aqueous solutions of bases conduct electricity.
  • Bases feel soapy to touch.
Question - Why do bases turn red litmus blue?

Answer -

Bases turn red litmus paper blue because they produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in aqueous solution.

These hydroxide ions are responsible for the basic nature of bases.

Question - Why do bases feel soapy to touch?

Answer -

Bases feel soapy to touch because they react with the natural oils present on the skin and produce a slippery sensation.

Therefore, most bases have a characteristic soapy feel.

Question - Why do aqueous solutions of bases conduct electricity?

Answer -

Aqueous solutions of bases conduct electricity because they contain free ions.

When bases dissolve in water, they produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) and positive ions, which carry electric current.

Question - How do bases react with metals? Explain with an example.

Answer -

Some bases react with certain metals such as zinc and aluminium to form a salt and hydrogen gas.

Example:

2NaOH + Zn → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂↑

In this reaction, sodium zincate and hydrogen gas are formed.

Question - How do bases react with acids? Explain with an example.

Answer -

Bases react with acids to form salt and water.

This reaction is called a neutralisation reaction.

Example:

NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O

Question - How do bases react with non-metal oxides?

Answer -

Bases react with non-metal oxides to form salt and water.

Since non-metal oxides are acidic in nature, they react with bases in a manner similar to acids.

Example:

Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O

Question - What are indicators?

Answer -

Indicators are substances that help us identify whether a solution is acidic or basic by showing a change in colour or smell.

They are widely used in laboratories to test the nature of solutions.

Question - What are natural indicators? Give examples.

Answer -

Indicators obtained from natural sources such as plants are called natural indicators.

Examples:

  • Litmus
  • Turmeric
  • China Rose Indicator (Hibiscus)
  • Red Cabbage Extract
Question - What are synthetic indicators? Give examples.

Answer -

Indicators prepared artificially in laboratories are called synthetic indicators.

Examples:

  • Phenolphthalein
  • Methyl Orange
Question - What are olfactory indicators? Give examples.

Answer -

Indicators whose smell changes in acidic or basic media are called olfactory indicators.

Examples:

  • Onion
  • Vanilla Essence
  • Clove Oil
Question - What is a universal indicator?

Answer -

A universal indicator is a mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different pH values.

It is used to determine the approximate pH of a solution and to identify whether the solution is acidic, basic or neutral.

Question - What is pH? Who developed the pH scale?

Answer -

The term pH stands for the potential of hydrogen. It is a measure of the hydrogen ion (H⁺) concentration in a solution.

The pH scale was developed by S. P. L. Sørensen in 1909.

The pH value helps us determine whether a solution is acidic, basic or neutral.

Question - What is the range of the pH scale?

Answer -

The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14.

  • pH less than 7 indicates an acidic solution.
  • pH equal to 7 indicates a neutral solution.
  • pH greater than 7 indicates a basic solution.
Question - What are the pH values of acidic, basic and neutral solutions?

Answer -

  • Acidic Solution: pH less than 7
  • Neutral Solution: pH equal to 7
  • Basic Solution: pH greater than 7

The strength of acidity increases as the pH value decreases.

Question - Explain the relationship between pH value and hydrogen ion concentration.

Answer -

The pH value of a solution is inversely related to the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺).

  • Higher H⁺ concentration → Lower pH value
  • Lower H⁺ concentration → Higher pH value

Thus, a strong acid has a low pH value, while a strong base has a high pH value.

Question - Why is pH considered an important measure of acidity and basicity?

Answer -

The pH scale provides a simple numerical value to indicate the strength of acidic and basic solutions.

It helps compare different solutions and determine their nature accurately.

Question - Why is pH important in everyday life?

Answer -

pH plays an important role in many biological and chemical processes occurring in daily life.

  • Digestion in the stomach.
  • Prevention of tooth decay.
  • Self-defence by plants and animals.
  • Growth of crops in soil.
Question - Explain the role of pH in digestion and indigestion.

Answer -

The stomach produces hydrochloric acid (HCl), which helps in digestion.

Sometimes excess acid is produced in the stomach, causing indigestion and pain.

To relieve this discomfort, antacids such as magnesium hydroxide are used to neutralise the excess acid.

Question - How does pH affect tooth decay?

Answer -

Tooth enamel is damaged when the pH in the mouth falls below 5.5.

Acids produced by bacteria from food particles lower the pH and cause tooth decay.

Brushing with basic toothpaste helps neutralise these acids and protects the teeth.

Question - Explain the importance of pH in self-defence by plants and animals.

Answer -

Many plants and animals use acidic or basic substances for protection.

For example, a bee sting contains an acidic substance that causes pain and irritation.

The effect can be reduced by applying a mild base such as baking soda.

Similarly, some plants such as nettles release acidic substances for defence.

Question - Why is proper soil pH necessary for healthy plant growth?

Answer -

Plants grow best within a specific pH range.

If the soil becomes too acidic or too basic, plants cannot absorb nutrients properly.

Therefore, maintaining a suitable soil pH is essential for healthy plant growth and better crop production.
