📘 Unit 3 - Matels and non matels
Question - What are metals?

Answer -

Metals are elements that are generally hard, lustrous, malleable, ductile and good conductors of heat and electricity.

Metals usually lose electrons to form positive ions (cations).

Examples: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Aluminium (Al) and Gold (Au).

Question - Write the physical properties of metals.

Answer -

The physical properties of metals are:

  • Metals are generally solid at room temperature (except mercury).
  • Metals are lustrous (shiny).
  • Metals are malleable.
  • Metals are ductile.
  • Metals are good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Metals are sonorous.
  • Metals generally have high melting and boiling points.
  • Metals usually possess high density and strength.
Question - What is lustre?

Answer -

The property by which a metal has a shiny surface and reflects light is called lustre.

Examples: Gold, silver and copper are lustrous metals.

Question - What is malleability?

Answer -

The property by which metals can be beaten into thin sheets with the help of a hammer is called malleability.

Metals that show this property are called malleable metals.

Example: Gold and aluminium can be beaten into thin sheets.

Question - What is ductility?

Answer -

The property by which metals can be drawn into thin wires is called ductility.

Metals that show this property are called ductile metals.

Example: Copper and aluminium are used for making electrical wires because they are ductile.

Question - What is sonority?

Answer -

The property by which metals produce a ringing sound when struck with a hard object is called sonority.

Metals possessing this property are called sonorous metals.

This property is used in making bells and musical instruments.

Question - Discuss the exceptions in the physical properties of metals.

Answer -

Although metals generally show common physical properties, there are some exceptions:

  • Mercury is a metal but exists as a liquid at room temperature.
  • Sodium and potassium are very soft metals and can be cut with a knife.
  • Gallium and caesium have very low melting points.
  • Lead and mercury are comparatively poor conductors of heat.
Question - Name a metal which is liquid at room temperature.

Answer -

Mercury (Hg) is the only metal that exists in liquid state at room temperature.

Question - Name a metal which can be cut with a knife.

Answer -

Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K) are very soft metals and can be easily cut with a knife.

Question - Which metal is the best conductor of heat and electricity?

Answer -

Silver (Ag) is the best conductor of heat and electricity among all metals.

However, copper and aluminium are more commonly used because they are less expensive.

Question - Write the chemical properties of metals with chemical equations.

Answer -

Metals undergo various chemical reactions due to their tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions.

1. Reaction of Metals with Oxygen

Metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides.

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃

Most metal oxides are basic in nature.

2. Reaction of Metals with Water

Some metals react with water to form metal hydroxides or metal oxides and hydrogen gas.

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑

Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑

3Fe + 4H₂O (steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑

3. Reaction of Metals with Dilute Acids

Metals react with dilute acids to form salt and hydrogen gas.

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑

Mg + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂↑

Metals below hydrogen in the reactivity series do not react with dilute acids.

4. Reaction of Metals with Salt Solutions

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

5. Reaction of Metals with Chlorine

Metals react with chlorine to form metal chlorides.

2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl

2Fe + 3Cl₂ → 2FeCl₃

Question - Why do metals form basic oxides?

Answer -

Metal oxides react with acids to form salt and water. Therefore, most metal oxides are basic in nature.

Example:

MgO + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂O

CaO + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O

Question - What are amphoteric oxides? Give two examples.

Answer -

Oxides that react with both acids and bases to form salt and water are called amphoteric oxides.

Examples:

  • Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃)
  • Zinc oxide (ZnO)
Question - Why do metals displace other metals from their salt solutions?

Answer -

A more reactive metal has a greater tendency to lose electrons than a less reactive metal.

Therefore, a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Example:

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

Question - What is the reactivity series?

Answer -

The arrangement of metals in the order of their decreasing reactivity is called the reactivity series.

The reactivity series is:

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au

A metal higher in the series can displace a metal lower in the series from its compounds.

Question - Why does iron displace copper from copper sulphate solution?

Answer -

Iron is more reactive than copper and is placed above copper in the reactivity series.

Therefore, iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution to form iron sulphate and copper metal.

Chemical equation:

Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu

The blue colour of copper sulphate solution gradually changes to green due to the formation of iron sulphate.

Question - Why does zinc displace copper from copper sulphate solution?

Answer -

Zinc is more reactive than copper and is placed above copper in the reactivity series.

Therefore, zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

Chemical equation:

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

Copper gets deposited and zinc sulphate solution is formed.

Question - What happens when zinc is added to iron(II) sulphate solution?

Answer -

Zinc is more reactive than iron.

When zinc is added to iron(II) sulphate solution, it displaces iron from the solution and forms zinc sulphate.

Chemical equation:

Zn + FeSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Fe

This is an example of a displacement reaction.

Question - Why can copper not displace zinc from zinc sulphate solution?

Answer -

Copper is less reactive than zinc and is placed below zinc in the reactivity series.

A less reactive metal cannot displace a more reactive metal from its salt solution.

Therefore, copper cannot displace zinc from zinc sulphate solution.

Question - Why is hydrogen included in the reactivity series?

Answer -

Hydrogen is included in the reactivity series as a reference element.

It helps to compare the reactivity of metals and determine whether a metal can displace hydrogen from dilute acids.

Metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series can liberate hydrogen gas from dilute acids, whereas metals below hydrogen cannot.

Question - Why is sodium immersed in kerosene oil?

Answer -

Sodium is a highly reactive metal. It reacts vigorously with oxygen and moisture present in air and may catch fire.

To prevent its reaction with air and water, sodium is stored under kerosene oil.

Kerosene does not react with sodium and acts as a protective layer.

Question - Why are sodium, potassium and lithium stored under oil?

Answer -

Sodium, potassium and lithium are highly reactive metals.

They react rapidly with oxygen and moisture present in air and may catch fire.

Therefore, they are stored under kerosene oil to prevent contact with air and moisture.

Question - Why are sodium, potassium, calcium and magnesium not found in the free state in nature?

Answer -

Sodium, potassium, calcium and magnesium are highly reactive metals.

They readily react with oxygen, water and other substances present in the environment.

Therefore, they are not found in the free state and occur in the form of compounds.

Question - What are noble metals? Give two examples.

Answer -

Metals which are very less reactive and do not react easily with air, water or dilute acids are called noble metals.

Examples:

  • Gold (Au)
  • Platinum (Pt)

Silver (Ag) is also considered a noble metal.

Question - Why are gold and platinum found in the free state in nature?

Answer -

Gold and platinum are very unreactive metals.

They do not react readily with oxygen, water or other substances present in nature.

Therefore, they are found in the free state (native state) in nature.

Question - What are basic oxides? Give two examples.

Answer -

Oxides of metals that react with acids to form salt and water are called basic oxides.

Examples:

  • Magnesium oxide (MgO)
  • Calcium oxide (CaO)

Example reaction:

MgO + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂O

Question - What are acidic oxides? Give two examples.

Answer -

Oxides of non-metals that react with bases to form salt and water are called acidic oxides.

Examples:

  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • Sulphur dioxide (SO₂)

Example reaction:

CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O

Question - What are amphoteric oxides? Give two examples.

Answer -

Oxides that react with both acids and bases to form salt and water are called amphoteric oxides.

Examples:

  • Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃)
  • Zinc oxide (ZnO)
Question - Show that aluminium oxide is amphoteric in nature.

Answer -

Aluminium oxide reacts with both acids and bases. Therefore, it is an amphoteric oxide.

Reaction with acid:

Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O

Reaction with base:

Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO₂ + H₂O

Question - Show that zinc oxide is amphoteric in nature.

Answer -

Zinc oxide reacts with both acids and bases. Therefore, it is an amphoteric oxide.

Reaction with acid:

ZnO + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂O

Reaction with base:

ZnO + 2NaOH → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂O

Question - What are minerals?

Answer -

Naturally occurring elements or compounds present in the Earth's crust are called minerals.

Examples: Bauxite, Hematite, Zinc blende, Limestone etc.

Question - What are ores?

Answer -

Minerals from which metals can be extracted conveniently and economically are called ores.

All ores are minerals, but all minerals are not ores.

Examples:

  • Bauxite – Ore of Aluminium
  • Hematite – Ore of Iron
  • Zinc Blende – Ore of Zinc
Question - What is gangue?

Answer -

The unwanted impurities such as sand, soil, clay and rocky materials present along with an ore are called gangue.

Gangue is removed before the extraction of metal.

Question - Differentiate between minerals and ores.

Answer -

Minerals Ores
Minerals are naturally occurring substances present in the Earth's crust. Ores are minerals from which metals can be extracted economically.
All minerals do not contain sufficient metal. Ores contain a high percentage of metal.
Extraction of metal may not be economical. Extraction of metal is economical.
Question - Why are only some minerals called ores?

Answer -

Only those minerals which contain a sufficiently high percentage of a metal and from which the metal can be extracted economically are called ores.

Therefore, all minerals are not considered ores.

Question - Why are carbonate and sulphide ores converted into oxides before reduction?

Answer -

Metals can be extracted more easily from their oxides than from their sulphides or carbonates.

Therefore, carbonate ores and sulphide ores are first converted into metal oxides and then reduced to obtain the metal.

This makes the extraction process easier and more economical.

Question - What is calcination? Give an example.

Answer -

The process of heating a carbonate ore strongly in the absence or limited supply of air is called calcination.

During calcination, the carbonate ore is converted into metal oxide and carbon dioxide gas is released.

Example:

ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂

Question - What is roasting? Give an example.

Answer -

The process of heating a sulphide ore strongly in the presence of excess air or oxygen is called roasting.

During roasting, the sulphide ore is converted into metal oxide and sulphur dioxide gas is released.

Example:

2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂

Question - Differentiate between calcination and roasting.

Answer -

Calcination Roasting
Used for carbonate ores. Used for sulphide ores.
Heating is done in the absence or limited supply of air. Heating is done in the presence of excess air.
Carbon dioxide gas is released. Sulphur dioxide gas is released.
Example: ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂ Example: 2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂
Question - Why is the reduction of metal oxides easier than sulphides and carbonates?

Answer -

Metal oxides can be reduced easily using reducing agents such as carbon or aluminium.

On the other hand, sulphides and carbonates cannot be reduced directly in an economical manner.

Therefore, sulphide and carbonate ores are first converted into oxides before reduction.
